Understanding macronutrients

Macronutrients — protein, carbohydrates, and fat — are the three main sources of energy in food. Each plays a distinct role in the body, and getting the right balance for your goals can significantly affect your performance, body composition, and energy levels.

Protein (4 kcal/g)

Protein is essential for building and repairing muscle tissue, supporting immune function, and producing enzymes and hormones. For most active adults, a target of 1.6–2.2g per kg of body weight is sufficient for muscle building or preservation.

Carbohydrates (4 kcal/g)

Carbohydrates are the body's preferred fuel source, especially for high-intensity exercise. Not all carbs are equal — fiber-rich whole grains, vegetables, and legumes are far preferable to refined sugars and processed starches.

Fat (9 kcal/g)

Dietary fat is essential for hormone production, vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K), and brain health. The focus should be on unsaturated fats (olive oil, nuts, avocado) while minimizing trans fats.

Frequently asked questions

What are macros and why do they matter?
Macros (macronutrients) are the three main nutrient categories: protein, carbohydrates, and fat. Protein provides 4 kcal/g, carbohydrates 4 kcal/g, and fat 9 kcal/g. Tracking macros gives you more control over body composition than tracking calories alone — ensuring you get enough protein to preserve muscle during fat loss.
How much protein do I need per day?
For general health: 1.0–1.2g per kg of body weight. For fat loss or muscle building: 1.6–2.2g per kg. The official RDA of 0.8g/kg is the minimum to prevent deficiency — not an optimal target for anyone with health or fitness goals.
Should I count calories or macros?
For general weight management, calorie tracking is sufficient. For body composition goals — gaining muscle while minimising fat, or losing fat while preserving muscle — macro tracking provides more precision. Tracking protein specifically is the most impactful single change most people can make.
How do I adjust macros for muscle building?
For muscle building, prioritise protein at 1.6–2.2g/kg and eat in a slight calorie surplus (200–300 kcal above TDEE). Carbohydrates should make up the majority of the remaining calories to fuel training performance and recovery. Fat at 0.8–1.0g/kg is a reasonable minimum.
Individual needs vary significantly. These are evidence-based starting points. A registered dietitian can provide a truly personalized macro plan.
